My earliest memory of Weezer comes from 2001 when my mom, older brother and I first saw the “Hash Pipe” video on MTV. The title was censored to read “H--- Pipe,” so my mom sent my brother on a mission to discover what the blurred out word was. When he came back from school with news that it was “Hash Pipe,” we were all disappointed, hoping for something more risque.

At the age of seven, this was the first of many Weezer-related disappointments to come.

I was born in 1994, the year Weezer released The Blue Album, and like many others of this generation, I found it difficult to classify Weezer – one of the biggest rock bands of our birth period – as anything other than “cool.” The occupation of being in a band seemed to be what made rock stars cool, not the other way around. As a kid, the notion of an “uncool rock star” seemed like a contradiction, so Weezer was inherently cool.

But as I grew more familiar with music, it became obvious that there are, without a doubt, uncool rock stars. Weezer may in fact be the prototype for uncool rock stars.

What rock star other than Rivers Cuomo has (a) declared that he is “Tired Of Sex,” (b) gone to Harvard, and (c) traded in a life of sex, drugs and rock ‘n’ roll for Shakespeare, celibacy and meditation?

Today, when we speak of Weezer, we are usually talking about what transpired between 1994-2002. We seem to ignore the unignorable: a decade-long dry spell of albums that are patently bad, even for a band that embraces that sort of thing.

This is why I was so shocked to see the music world’s coverage of Weezer’s two newest singles, “Thank God For Girls” and “Do You Wanna Get High?” elevated to an abnormal level of acceptance. Within hours of their releases, Rolling Stone had posts about each single’s video on Twitter and Facebook, and a brief feature article about the backstory of “Do You Wanna Get High?”

It seems as if Weezer is back. But can it really be true? Sure the singles were both decent, but are fans really ready to forgive Hurley, Raditude, The Red album and Make Believe?

In researching this story, I conducted a number of interviews, and everyone shared a genuine air of excitement for simply being asked to talk about Weezer. It is a band with a downright passionate fan base, and the rare breed of band in which everybody, fan or not, seems to have a story. Generally the biggest fans are the harshest critics, so talking to declared Weezer fans seemed to be the most promising research avenue.
